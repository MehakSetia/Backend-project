Great Himalayan Range

The Great Himalaya Range, spanning around 1,500 miles, is a monumental mountain range in Asia that includes some of the tallest peaks on Earth, including Mount Everest and Kangchenjunga. Formed by the tectonic collision of the Indian and Eurasian plates, it influences weather patterns, provides vital freshwater resources, and is rich in biodiversity. The range is culturally significant, with many important Hindu and Buddhist sites and diverse ethnic communities.

Shivalik Range

The Shivalik Range, also known as the Siwaliks, is the outermost range of the Himalayas, stretching across northern India, Nepal, and Pakistan. It lies between the Lesser Himalayas and the Indo-Gangetic Plain. The Shivaliks are typically rising between 1,000 and 2,000 meters. This range is known for its rolling hills, fertile valleys, and rich biodiversity, and it plays a key role in forming the transition zone between the high Himalayas and the plains.

Vindhya Range

The Vindhya Range is a mountain range in central India, extending approximately 1,000 miles from the east to the west.It forms a natural barrier between the northern and southern parts of India, running parallel to the Narmada River. The range is typically ranging from 300 to 1,000 meters. The Vindhyas play a significant role in India's climate and hydrology, influencing the distribution of rivers and contributing to the region's diverse ecosystems.

Aravalli Range

The Aravalli Range is one of the oldest mountain ranges in India, extending about 800 kilometers from the northeastern state of Gujarat to the southeastern state of Haryana. It forms a natural barrier between the Thar Desert to the west and the fertile plains of northern India to the east. The range features low to moderate elevations. Western Ghats

The Western Ghats, also known as the Sahyadri Hills, are a mountain range running parallel to the western coast of India. Extending about 1,600 kilometers from Gujarat to Tamil Nadu, they are known for their rich biodiversity, with numerous endemic species of flora and fauna.The Western Ghats play a crucial role in influencing India's monsoon weather patterns and are recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site for their ecological importance.
